How Much Does a Tattoo Artist Website Cost in 2026?

By GetMinted · March 9, 2026

Ask around and you'll get wildly different answers. A freelancer quotes you $2,000. An agency sends a proposal for $8,000. Someone on YouTube says just use Squarespace for $23/month. A friend swears by Wix. Meanwhile, your Instagram is doing fine and you're not sure any of this is worth it.

The truth is that most tattoo artists are being sold two options that are both wrong for them: a custom site that costs more than it should, or a DIY tool that eats 20+ hours of your time and still looks generic. In 2026, there's a better path. But first — let's break down what you're actually looking at with each option.

Option 1: DIY Website Builders (Squarespace, Wix, etc.)

Squarespace / Wix / Weebly

$16–$50/month · 20+ hours to set up · No SEO included

These tools give you a drag-and-drop editor and a library of templates. The pitch is that anyone can build a site in an afternoon. The reality: you'll spend a weekend fighting the editor, trying to make a generic template look like you, and realizing the free plan doesn't let you use a custom domain.

The bigger problem isn't the time — it's that these platforms aren't built for local SEO. They won't automatically optimize your title tags, set up your schema markup, or make sure Google can find you when someone searches "tattoo artist [your city]." You'd need to figure all of that out separately, which most artists never do.

By the time you're paying $23–$50/month for a real plan, adding a custom domain, and potentially hiring someone to set up SEO, you've spent hundreds of dollars and dozens of hours — and you still have a site that looks like a Squarespace template.

Option 2: Hiring a Freelancer

Freelance Web Designer

$800–$5,000 upfront · 2–4 weeks turnaround · SEO usually not included

A good freelancer can build you a genuinely custom site that matches your brand. The problem: you pay everything upfront, you wait weeks for the build, and most freelancers aren't SEO specialists — so unless you specifically pay extra for it, your site won't rank on Google.

Freelancer quality varies enormously. At $800 you're getting a template with your photos swapped in. At $3,000–$5,000 you might get something genuinely custom. And when you need updates — new photos, a new booking link, a page change — you're either waiting on them or paying more.

For a tattoo artist who just wants a site that books clients, the freelancer model has a lot of friction for the price.

Option 3: Hiring an Agency

Web Design Agency

$3,000–$15,000 upfront · Ongoing retainer often required · Overkill for most artists

Agencies are built for businesses with complex needs — multiple service lines, large teams, e-commerce, enterprise software integrations. For a tattoo artist who needs a portfolio, a booking link, and Google visibility, an agency is like hiring a construction crew to build a bookshelf.

That said, agencies do deliver results — if you're at the scale where a $10K website investment makes sense for your revenue. For most independent tattoo artists, it simply isn't.
