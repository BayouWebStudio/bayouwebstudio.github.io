Artist Spotlight: Carlos Reyes

By GetMinted · March 9, 2026 · Houston, TX

Carlos Reyes doesn't do color. He doesn't need to. Working entirely in black and grey, Carlos has carved out a reputation in Houston as one of the most technically precise realism artists in the city — portraits that look like photographs, dark art that hits you in the chest, and fine-detail work that holds up long after the session ends.

Seven years in, with around 8K followers on Instagram under @carlosreyesink, Carlos runs a tight operation: selective clientele, intentional subject matter, and a site through GetMinted that handles the logistics so he can focus on the work. We sat down with him to talk craft, clients, and why he thinks most tattoo artists are sleeping on their own potential.

How did you land on black and grey realism?

"Honestly, it chose me more than I chose it," Carlos says. "I started tattooing doing whatever clients asked for — color work, traditional, script. But every time I did a portrait or something realistic, people stopped and stared. That told me something."

The shift to black and grey came naturally. Carlos found that stripping color out of the equation forced him to get better at everything that actually makes realism work — value, contrast, smooth transitions, the texture of skin versus cloth versus shadow. "Color can hide a lot of weakness. Black and grey doesn't let you hide anything. That's what I like about it."

He's now known specifically for portrait realism and dark art — skulls, religious iconography, cinematic horror imagery, memorial pieces. The subject matter is heavy, but his approach is precise and deliberate. "People come to me with the most meaningful things in their lives. A parent. A memory. A feeling they can't put words to. I take that seriously."

What does your typical client look like now versus when you started?

"Night and day. Early on I'd tattoo anyone — birthday script, tribal bands, whatever paid the week. Nothing wrong with that, it's how you learn. But now I get people who have been thinking about a piece for years. They've saved up, they've done their research, they know what they want. Those are my people."

Carlos attributes part of that shift to how he presents himself online. "When your Instagram only shows one style, one vibe, one level of work — clients self-select. The people who aren't right for you don't bother reaching out. That alone is worth a thousand posts."

"Black and grey realism isn't a style. It's a discipline. Every piece is a test of how well you understand light, form, and patience. I'm still learning — I expect to be learning for the next twenty years."

What's something about tattooing that most people outside the industry don't understand?

"How much of this job is psychology," Carlos says without hesitation. "Before I put a needle anywhere, I'm reading the room. Is this person nervous? Do they actually know what they want, or are they going off an impulse? Are they going to flinch in a spot where I need a steady surface? The tattoo is the last 20% of the job."

That human element is part of what keeps him hooked. "I've done portraits of people's kids, their parents who passed, their dogs. Those sessions don't feel like work. They feel like something that matters. That's why I'm still here seven years in and still excited to come in every day."

You use GetMinted for your site — how has that changed things?

"Before I had a site, everything was DMs and word of mouth. Which worked, but it was chaos. People asking the same questions over and over. No real system. I'd lose track of inquiries."

His GetMinted site changed the intake process completely. "I put my style, my rates, what I do and don't take, and a booking form that requires a deposit. Now when someone comes in, they already know everything. There's no negotiating, no surprises. It filters out the tire-kickers automatically."

He's also noticed more clients finding him through Google. "I get inquiries now from people searching for black and grey realism in Houston. They're not following me — they just found me through search. That's a different kind of client. More serious, usually."
